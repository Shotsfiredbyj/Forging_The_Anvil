## 1. Lean Canvas

**Origin:** Ash Maurya, adapted from Osterwalder's Business Model Canvas for the Lean Startup methodology. Designed to be completed in under 20 minutes on one page.

### The 9 Fields

| # | Field | What It Captures | Key Question |
|---|-------|-----------------|--------------|
| 1 | **Customer Segments** | Who pays (customers) vs. who uses (users). Early adopters specifically, not mainstream. | "Who has this problem most acutely? Who would pay to solve it today?" |
| 2 | **Problem** | Top 1-3 most pressing problems. Existing alternatives customers currently use. | "What are the top 3 problems your target customer faces? How do they solve them now?" |
| 3 | **Unique Value Proposition** | Single, clear, compelling message. Under 120 characters. Connects to the #1 problem. | "Why is your product different and worth paying attention to? What's it for and who's it for?" |
| 4 | **Solution** | The simplest thing you could build to address each problem. Intentionally kept small. | "What is the minimum feature set to address the top problems?" |
| 5 | **Channels** | How you reach early adopters. Both initial and scalable channels. | "How will you find and reach your first customers?" |
| 6 | **Revenue Streams** | Pricing model. Price as part of the product. | "How will you make money? What will customers pay?" |
| 7 | **Cost Structure** | MVP development costs, burn rate, 3-6 month runway needs. | "What will it cost to build this and operate for the first 6 months?" |
| 8 | **Key Metrics** | 3-5 metrics measuring business performance. Leading indicators preferred. | "What 3-5 numbers tell you if this is working?" |
| 9 | **Unfair Advantage** | What cannot be easily copied or bought. Insider knowledge, network effects, etc. | "What do you have that a well-funded competitor cannot easily replicate?" |

### Recommended Fill Order

Ash Maurya's position has evolved. His current stance: **there is no fixed fill order for idea generation** — start wherever feels natural and take a quick snapshot. However, for **validation**, there IS an order: prioritise testing your riskiest assumptions first.

**Maurya's practical guidance:**
- At the earliest stage, focus on just **Customer Segment** and **Problem** — the two outer boxes
- When starting validation, fill in Problem, Customer Segment, and UVP, **leaving Solution intentionally blank**
- The solution box should stay small — aligned with MVP thinking
- Leave Unfair Advantage for last (hardest, and often blank initially — that's fine)

**Why this order matters:** Most founders start with a solution and backfill the problem. This order forces problem-first thinking. Leaving Solution blank during early validation prevents the common trap of writing problem statements that merely justify a pre-decided solution.

### Minimum Viable Extraction

For a raw idea, the minimum viable Lean Canvas needs:
1. **Customer Segment** — who specifically
2. **Problem** — what specifically, and how they solve it now
3. **UVP** — why this is different (even roughly)

Everything else can be "I don't know yet" and that's the point.

## 2. Business Model Canvas

**Origin:** Alexander Osterwalder, 2005 PhD work, popularised in *Business Model Generation* (2010).

### The 9 Building Blocks

**Right side (value delivery):**
- **Customer Segments** — For whom are we creating value?
- **Value Propositions** — What value do we deliver to the customer?
- **Channels** — How do we reach our customer segments?
- **Customer Relationships** — What type of relationship does each segment expect?
- **Revenue Streams** — For what value are customers willing to pay?

**Left side (infrastructure):**
- **Key Resources** — What key resources does our value proposition require?
- **Key Activities** — What key things do we do to deliver the value proposition?
- **Key Partnerships** — Who are our key partners and suppliers?
- **Cost Structure** — What are the most important costs inherent in our business model?

### Why Lean Canvas Differs

Maurya replaced Key Partners, Key Activities, Key Resources, and Customer Relationships with **Problem, Solution, Key Metrics, and Unfair Advantage**. His rationale: for early-stage startups, understanding the problem and measuring progress matters more than mapping infrastructure you haven't built yet.

### Relevance to Cold Anvil

The full BMC is better for established businesses analysing their model. For raw idea extraction, the Lean Canvas is the right variant. However, the BMC's emphasis on **Customer Relationships** (what kind of relationship does the customer expect?) is worth borrowing — it's absent from Lean Canvas but matters for product vision.

## 3. Design Thinking — Define Phase

**Origin:** Stanford d.school / IDEO. The "Define" phase is stage 2 of the 5-stage model (Empathize → Define → Ideate → Prototype → Test).

### What the Define Phase Does

Takes raw empathy data (observations, interviews, immersion) and synthesises it into an actionable problem statement. The goal is **clarity and focus** — turning vague understanding into a specific, human-centred problem worth solving.

### The Point of View (POV) Statement

**Template:**
> **[User]** needs a way to **[need]** because **[insight]**

Three elements must be present:
1. **User** — a specific person or archetype (not "users" generically)
2. **Need** — expressed as a verb, not a solution (not "needs an app" but "needs to track...")
3. **Insight** — the surprising or non-obvious reason why this matters

**Characteristics of a good POV:**
- Human-centred (about people, not technology)
- Broad enough for creative freedom
- Narrow enough to be actionable
- Contains a genuine insight, not just a restatement of the problem

**Example:**
> "A busy first-time mother needs a way to feel confident she's feeding her toddler well because conflicting nutrition advice creates anxiety that undermines her parenting confidence."

NOT: "Parents need a meal planning app because cooking is hard."

### How Might We (HMW) Questions

Once the POV is established, it's converted into generative questions:

> "How might we [design opportunity]?"

**Rules for good HMW questions:**
- Broad enough for a wide range of solutions
- Narrow enough that specific solutions can be created
- Don't embed a solution in the question
- Start from the insight, not the obvious problem

**Example progression:**
- POV: New remote workers need to feel connected to their team because isolation erodes motivation
- HMW: "How might we create moments of genuine connection for remote workers without adding meeting fatigue?"

### Synthesis Tools

- **Empathy Map** — 4 quadrants: Say, Do, Think, Feel
- **Space Saturation** — literally covering a wall with observations to find patterns
- **Affinity Clustering** — grouping observations to find themes

### Key Extraction Questions (for conversational use)

1. "Who specifically has this problem?"
2. "What are they trying to do when the problem occurs?"
3. "What do they currently do about it?"
4. "How does it make them feel?"
5. "What would 'solved' look like from their perspective?"
6. "What's the non-obvious reason this matters to them?"

### Why This Order Matters

You can't define a meaningful problem without empathy data. And you can't ideate solutions without a well-defined problem. The d.school sequence — empathize first, then define — prevents the single most common failure mode: defining a problem that's actually a restatement of your preferred solution.

## 4. Y Combinator / Accelerator Intake

### What YC Asks (Optimised Over Thousands of Startups)

These questions have been refined through evaluating tens of thousands of applications. They extract signal from noise with remarkable efficiency.

#### The 40 YC Questions (Molfar.io compilation)

**Problem (10 questions):**
1. What problem are you solving?
2. What problem will be solved at the end of what you are doing?
3. What do we expect the result to be?
4. Can you state the problem clearly in two sentences?
5. Have you experienced the problem yourself?
6. Can you define this problem narrowly?
7. Who can you help first?
8. What can we address immediately?
9. How do we get the first indication this thing is working?
10. Is the problem solvable?

**Customer (9 questions):**
11. Who is your customer?
12. Who is the ideal first customer?
13. How will they know if your product has solved the problem?
14. How often does your user have the problem?
15. Who is getting the most value out of your product?
16. How intense is the problem?
17. Are they willing to pay?
18. How easy is it for your customer to find your product?
19. Which customers should you run away from?

**Product (6 questions):**
20. Does your product actually solve the problem? Be truthful. How and why not?
21. Which customers should you go after first?
22. How do you find people willing to use your "bad" first versions?
23. Who are the most desperate customers? How do you talk to them first?
24. Whose business is going out of business without using you?
25. Are you discounting or starting with a super low price?

**Performance (7 questions):**
26. What are you using to measure how users interact with your product?
27. What 5-10 metrics measure how your product functions?
28. When building new features, what metric improves because of it?
29. What number shows how well your company is doing?
30. What is your top-level KPI?
31. What underlying metrics contribute to your top-level KPI?
32. Which metrics are you trying to move this cycle?

**Product Development (8 questions):**
33. How long is your product dev cycle?
34-40. [Operational questions about dev process, prioritisation, spec writing]

#### YC Interview Core Questions

The 10-minute interview typically covers:
1. "What does your company do?" (must be 1-2 sentences, no jargon)
2. "How does it work?"
3. "Who are your users and how do you get them?"
4. "What makes new users try you? Why do reluctant ones hold back?"
5. "How much are your users using the product? Do they stick around?"
6. "How do you make money? How much could you make?"
7. "What's your growth like?"
8. "What do you know about this space that others don't?"
9. "What's the biggest risk?"
10. "Why now?"

#### YC Application Key Fields

- Describe what your company does in one or two sentences
- How many active users do you have?
- Anything notable about revenue/growth
- What progress have you made?
- Describe a time you successfully hacked something

### Techstars Application Questions

1. What problem is your company looking to solve?
2. What is your company going to make to solve this problem?
3. Who is your target audience?
4. What makes your team a winning team?
5. Share any traction you have
6. How do you (or will you) make money? How much could you make?
7. Who are your competitors or could become your competitors?
8. What is your competitive advantage?
9. Why is now the right time for your startup?
10. What is your customer acquisition strategy?
11. What are your next major company milestones?

### What Accelerators Prioritise (in order)

**Techstars:** "Team, team, team, market, product, traction" — team first because at the early stage, the team's ability to execute is the strongest predictor.

**YC:** Clarity of thought (can you explain it simply?), domain expertise (do you know the space better than anyone?), commitment (are you full-time?), execution evidence (have you made measurable progress?).

### Why This Matters for Cold Anvil

Accelerator intake questions are the result of evolutionary pressure — they've been optimised to extract maximum signal from minimal interaction. The YC interview is 10 minutes. If they can evaluate an idea in 10 minutes, those 10 minutes' worth of questions represent the minimum viable extraction.

## 5. Jobs To Be Done (JTBD)

**Origin:** Clayton Christensen (Harvard Business School), further developed by Bob Moesta and Tony Ulwick (Strategyn).

### Core Concept

People don't buy products — they "hire" them to do a job. A "job" is the progress a person is trying to make in a particular circumstance. Every job has three dimensions:

1. **Functional** — the practical task to be accomplished
2. **Social** — how the person wants to be perceived by others
3. **Emotional** — how the person wants to feel

### The Forces of Progress Model

Four forces drive every switching decision:

| Force | Direction | Question |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| **Push of current situation** | Drives change | "What's frustrating about how you do this today?" |
| **Pull of new solution** | Drives change | "What attracted you to this new approach?" |
| **Habit of existing behaviour** | Resists change | "What keeps you doing it the old way?" |
| **Anxiety about new solution** | Resists change | "What worries you about trying something new?" |

### JTBD Interview Questions

**The Switch Timeline** (understanding the moment someone decides to change):

1. "When did you first realise you needed something to solve this problem?"
2. "What were you trying to accomplish when that happened?"
3. "What time of day was it? Were you at home or at work? Who were you with?" (contextual memory triggers)
4. "What other solutions did you consider?"
5. "What were you trying to find when considering those solutions?"
6. "What other solutions have you actually used?"
7. "What did and didn't you like about them?"
8. "If this product/service wasn't available, what would you do instead?"
9. "Did you talk to anyone else about choices you were considering? What was that conversation like?"
10. "What did you imagine using this would be like?"
11. "Did you have any anxiety about purchasing/using it?"

**The "Fired Product" Question:**
- "What did you use before you switched? Why did you fire it?"

**The 5 Whys Technique:**
- Repeatedly ask "why?" to drill past surface needs to the core job

### Job Mapping

A job map breaks the job into 8 universal steps:
1. **Define** — what do they need to figure out before starting?
2. **Locate** — what inputs do they need to gather?
3. **Prepare** — how do they set up?
4. **Confirm** — how do they verify readiness?
5. **Execute** — the core activity
6. **Monitor** — how do they track progress?
7. **Modify** — how do they adjust?
8. **Conclude** — how do they finish and evaluate?

Each step is an opportunity to create value. The average job has 10-20 steps.

### Desired Outcome Statements

Structured as: **[Direction] + [metric] + [context]**

Example: "Minimise the time it takes to identify which customer segment to target first"

### Key Extraction for Cold Anvil

The JTBD lens for idea extraction:
1. "What job is your user hiring this product to do?"
2. "What's the functional outcome they need?"
3. "How do they want to feel when they've accomplished it?"
4. "How do they want to be seen by others?"
5. "What are they using today to get this job done?"
6. "What's broken or frustrating about the current approach?"

## 6. Product Discovery — Torres & Cagan

### Teresa Torres: Opportunity Solution Trees

**Structure (top to bottom):**

1. **Outcome** — the measurable business result the team is trying to achieve
2. **Opportunities** — customer needs, pain points, and desires (unmet)
3. **Solutions** — ideas/features that could address each opportunity
4. **Assumption Tests** — experiments to validate whether solutions work

**Key principles:**
- Start with a teeny tiny opportunity — break big problems into smaller, solvable ones
- Smaller opportunities → smaller solutions → faster testing
- Use story-based customer interviews: "Tell me about the last time when..."
- Conduct 3-4 interviews, map findings, then repeat
- The question "Is there more than one way to address this?" tests whether something is truly an opportunity (if there's only one way, it's a disguised solution)

**Discovery through conversation:**
- Never ask "What do you want?" — ask "Tell me about the last time..."
- Stories naturally reveal unmet needs and provide context
- Look for patterns across 3-4 stories before mapping

### Marty Cagan: Product Vision (from *Inspired* and *Empowered*)

**10 Principles for Product Vision:**

1. **Start with why** — articulate your purpose; everything follows from that
2. **Fall in love with the problem, not the solution**
3. **Think big** — visions are too often not ambitious enough
4. **Skate to where the puck is heading** — identify what's changing and what won't change
5. **Be customer-centric** — centre on the value you'll add to the customer's life
6. **Be inspiring** — teams need to connect with purpose
7. **Be differentiated** — show how your impact differs from competitors
8. **Embrace relevant trends** — but be thoughtful about which ones actually matter
9. **Make it a collaborative effort** — vision created in isolation lacks buy-in
10. **Iterate** — the first version won't be right; evolve it

**Product Vision Definition:**
> The future you are trying to create, typically 2-5 years out for software, 10 years for hardware. Most importantly: how this vision will improve the lives of your customers.

**Product Strategy:**
> The sequence of products or releases planned to deliver on the vision, structured around a series of product/market fits.

**Discovery vs. Delivery:**
- Discovery = figuring out what to build (testing ideas quickly and cheaply)
- Delivery = actually building it well
- Most companies over-invest in delivery and under-invest in discovery
- Empowered teams are assigned **problems to solve**, not features to build

### Key Extraction for Cold Anvil

From Cagan, the vision document needs:
1. **The future state** — what does the world look like when this product exists?
2. **The customer's life improvement** — how specifically are they better off?
3. **The differentiation** — why this, not something already available?
4. **The trend or timing** — what makes now the right moment?
5. **The ambition** — is this vision big enough to be worth pursuing?

## 7. Amazon Working Backwards (PR/FAQ)

**Origin:** Developed at Amazon circa 2004. Most major Amazon products since then (AWS, Kindle, Prime Video) were created through this process.

### The Press Release Structure

| Section | What It Forces You to Articulate |
|---------|--------------------------------|
| **Headline** | Product name + customer benefit in one sentence |
| **Subheading** | Specific customer segment + their key benefit |
| **Summary Paragraph** | What the product is, who it's for, why it matters (2-3 sentences) |
| **Problem Paragraph** | Customer pain points from THEIR perspective. Market size. Willingness to pay. |
| **Solution Paragraph(s)** | How the product solves the problem. Competitive differentiation. |
| **Leader Quote** | Internal spokesperson articulating why this matters |
| **Customer Quote** | Hypothetical testimonial — what a real customer would say |
| **Call to Action** | How to get started / where to find it |

### The FAQ Structure

**External FAQs (customer-facing):**
- How does the product work?
- What benefits can I expect?
- How much does it cost?
- When will it be available?
- How do I get started?
- What's the return/cancellation policy?

**Internal FAQs (stakeholder-facing):**
- What products or solutions does the target customer use today?
- What problem(s) does this product solve for customers?
- How does this product create value for customers?
- In what way(s) is this better, cheaper, or faster than alternatives?
- Who are the current competitors?
- How large is the estimated consumer demand / TAM?
- How many consumers have this need?
- What are the main risks and challenges?
- How does this align with our current offerings?
- What additional resources will be required?
- What customer data supports this approach?
- How will we measure success?
- What are the top 3 risks of failure?
- What are the unit economics?
- What's the timeline to profitability?

### Why Working Backwards Works

1. **Forces customer-centricity** — you write as if the customer is reading, not your boss
2. **Cheap to iterate** — "Drafting a press release is a lot easier and requires far fewer resources than building a minimum viable product"
3. **Exposes vagueness** — fuzzy thinking is immediately visible in prose
4. **Drives alignment** — everyone reads the same document (not slides with different interpretations)
5. **Tests the pitch** — if the press release isn't compelling, the product probably isn't either

### The Iterative Process

- Initial draft: solo effort by PM (several hours)
- 5+ rounds of revision typical
- Each round sharpens: "Is the customer clearly defined? Is the problem clearly defined? Does the proposed solution address the problem?"
- The test: would a customer change their behaviour to adopt this?

### Key Extraction for Cold Anvil

The PR/FAQ forces answers to:
1. Who is the customer? (specific segment)
2. What problem do they have? (from their perspective)
3. What's the product? (simple description)
4. Why is it better than what exists?
5. What would a customer say about it?
6. What would a skeptical stakeholder ask?

## 8. Problem-Solution Fit — Maurya & Fitzpatrick

### Ash Maurya: Running Lean

**Three stages of startup development:**
1. **Problem/Solution Fit** — "Do you have a problem worth solving?"
2. **Product/Market Fit** — "Have you built something people want?"
3. **Scale** — "How do you grow it?"

**The key insight:** Most startups fail not because they can't build the product, but because they build something nobody wants. Therefore, validate the PROBLEM before building the SOLUTION.

**Maurya's validation sequence:**
1. **Start with "What is your why?"** — the deeper motivation
2. **Document your Plan A** on a Lean Canvas (20 minutes)
3. **Identify your riskiest assumption** — usually "Does the customer have this problem?"
4. **Problem interviews** — validate the problem WITHOUT pitching a solution
5. **Solution interviews** — only after problem is validated, test whether your solution resonates
6. **Demo-Sell-Build** (not Build-Demo-Sell) — build a demo, see if you can sell it, THEN build

**Problem Interview questions:**
- What's the hardest part about [context]?
- Tell me about the last time you encountered this problem
- Why was that hard?
- What did you do to solve it?
- What don't you love about that solution?
- How often do you encounter this problem?
- How much time/money do you spend dealing with it?

### Rob Fitzpatrick: The Mom Test

**The three rules:**
1. Talk about their life, not your idea
2. Ask about specifics in the past, not generics or opinions about the future
3. Talk less and listen more

**Good questions (past behaviour, specific):**
- "What's the hardest part about [doing this thing]?"
- "Tell me about the last time that happened"
- "What did you do about it?"
- "What else have you tried?"
- "How are you dealing with it now?"
- "What don't you love about your current solution?"
- "Where does the money come from for this?" (who pays)
- "Who else should I talk to?"
- "Is there anything else I should be asking?"

**Bad questions (hypothetical, opinion-seeking):**
- "Would you use a product that does X?" (invites optimism)
- "How much would you pay for X?" (hypothetical = worthless)
- "Do you think this is a good idea?" (invites compliments)
- "Would you buy this?" (everyone says yes, few actually do)

**The key distinction:**
> "Would you use this?" tells you nothing. "When did you last face this problem?" tells you everything. The first invites optimism. The second demands memory.

**Three types of bad data to avoid:**
1. **Compliments** — "That sounds really cool!" (meaningless)
2. **Hypothetical fluff** — "I would definitely use that" (fiction)
3. **Wishlists** — "You should add X feature" (not a problem statement)

**Validation through commitments:**
The only real validation is when someone gives up something they value: time (they agree to a follow-up call), reputation (they introduce you to others), or money (they pre-order or sign a letter of intent).

## 9. Startup Pitch Structures

### Sequoia Capital Template

| Slide | What It Extracts | Key Question |
|-------|-----------------|--------------|
| **Company Purpose** | Single declarative sentence | "Define your company in one sentence" |
| **Problem** | Customer pain points | "What pain point are you addressing?" |
| **Solution** | The eureka moment | "Explain your eureka moment" |
| **Why Now** | Timing rationale | "Why hasn't this been built before? What changed?" |
| **Market Size** | TAM, SAM, SOM | "How big is the opportunity?" |
| **Competition** | Landscape and positioning | "Who else is doing this? Show you have a plan to win" |
| **Product** | What it does, how it works | "What are you actually building?" |
| **Business Model** | Revenue and profitability path | "How do you make money?" |
| **Team** | Founders and key people | "Why are you the right people?" |
| **Financials / Vision** | 5-year view | "What will you have built in 5 years?" |

### Guy Kawasaki 10/20/30

10 slides, 20 minutes, 30-point minimum font.

| Slide | Content |
|-------|---------|
| 1. Title | Company name, presenter, contact |
| 2. Problem | The pain you're solving |
| 3. Solution / Value Proposition | The direct value customers receive |
| 4. Underlying Magic / Competitive Advantage | Secret sauce — what differentiates you |
| 5. Business Model | How you make money |
| 6. Go-to-Market | Customer acquisition plan and costs |
| 7. Market Size | TAM and opportunity scope |
| 8. Team | Key people and their relevant experience |
| 9. Financials / Traction | Projections and progress to date |
| 10. Timeline / Vision | Where you've been, where you're going |

### What Investors Consistently Ask

Across all pitch frameworks, investors evaluate:
1. **Can I understand this in 3 minutes?** (clarity test)
2. **Is this a big market?** (opportunity test)
3. **Why will this team win?** (execution test)
4. **Why now?** (timing test)
5. **How do you make money?** (model test)
6. **What's the unfair advantage?** (defensibility test)

## 10. Cross-Framework Synthesis

### The Universal Extraction Set

Every serious framework, regardless of origin, extracts these elements. This is the **consensus minimum** for understanding an idea:

| Priority | Element | Who Asks For It |
|----------|---------|----------------|
| **1** | **Problem** — What pain exists? | ALL frameworks. Literally every single one starts here or puts it in the top 3. |
| **2** | **Customer** — Who has this problem? (specific) | Lean Canvas, YC, JTBD, Design Thinking, Pitch decks, PR/FAQ |
| **3** | **Current alternatives** — How do they solve it now? | JTBD, Mom Test, Lean Canvas, YC, Sequoia |
| **4** | **Solution** — What will you build? | All frameworks, but most insist on extracting this AFTER problem and customer |
| **5** | **Value proposition / differentiation** — Why is this better? | Lean Canvas, BMC, Pitch decks, PR/FAQ, Cagan |
| **6** | **Why now** — What changed that makes this possible/necessary? | Sequoia, Kawasaki, YC, Cagan ("skate to where the puck is heading") |
| **7** | **Business model** — How does money work? | Lean Canvas, BMC, Pitch decks, YC, Techstars |
| **8** | **Market size** — How big is the opportunity? | Pitch decks, PR/FAQ, YC, Techstars |
| **9** | **Unfair advantage / moat** — What's defensible? | Lean Canvas, Sequoia, Kawasaki |
| **10** | **Success metrics** — How do you know it's working? | Lean Canvas, YC, Torres (outcome at top of OST) |

### The Recommended Order

Based on cross-framework analysis, here is the optimal extraction sequence:

**Phase 1: The Problem Space (establish first, always)**
1. **The Problem** — What's broken? What hurts? What's frustrating?
2. **The Customer** — Who specifically experiences this? (not "everyone")
3. **Current Alternatives** — How do they cope today? What have they tried?
4. **Problem Severity** — How often? How painful? Would they pay to fix it?

**Phase 2: The Vision Space (only after problem is clear)**
5. **The Solution** — What would you build? (high level, not features)
6. **The Differentiation** — Why is this better than what exists?
7. **The Timing** — Why now? What changed?

**Phase 3: The Viability Space (grounds the vision)**
8. **Business Model** — How does money flow?
9. **Market Size** — How big is this?
10. **Go-to-Market** — How do you reach the first customers?
11. **Success Metrics** — What numbers tell you it's working?

**Phase 4: The Defensibility Space (the hardest, often last)**
12. **Unfair Advantage** — What's your moat?
13. **Key Risks** — What could kill this?
14. **Team/Founder Fit** — Why are you the right person to build this?

### Why This Order

1. **Problem before solution** — Every single framework agrees. Maurya, Fitzpatrick, Cagan, Torres, d.school, YC — all say: if you don't understand the problem, nothing else matters.

2. **Customer before solution** — You can't evaluate a solution without knowing who it's for. A "great solution" for the wrong customer is worthless. JTBD insists on understanding the person and their context before examining solutions.

3. **Current alternatives before your solution** — Fitzpatrick's Mom Test, JTBD's "fired product" question, and Lean Canvas's "existing alternatives" box all exist for the same reason: if you don't know what people do today, you can't know if your thing is better.

4. **Differentiation before business model** — PR/FAQ tests whether someone would change their behaviour for this. If the answer is "maybe, it's a bit better," the business model doesn't matter.

5. **Unfair advantage last** — Maurya explicitly says this is the hardest box and it's fine to leave it blank. Most founders at the idea stage don't have one yet, and pressuring them for one too early produces fiction.

## 11. Conversational Extraction Design

### How Experienced Practitioners Do This Through Conversation

The frameworks above were designed as written exercises or structured interviews. But the best practitioners — the ones who actually extract insight from half-formed ideas — do it through conversation. Here's how:

#### Start with the Story, Not the Pitch

**JTBD approach:** "Tell me about the last time you encountered this problem."
**Mom Test approach:** "What's the hardest part about [thing]?"
**Torres approach:** "Tell me about the last time when..."

The pattern: **get them telling a story, not pitching a solution**. Stories reveal problems, context, emotions, and severity that pitch decks hide.

#### Follow the Energy

Good interviewers notice where the founder's energy changes — where they lean in, where they get animated, where they get frustrated. That's usually where the real problem lives, even if they started the conversation with a different problem.

#### Ask "Why" More Than "What"

The 5 Whys from JTBD is the single most powerful extraction technique. Most founders describe their idea at the solution level. Five rounds of "why does that matter?" gets you to the actual job to be done.

#### Use Specific, Past-Tense Questions

Per Fitzpatrick: **"When did you last face this problem?"** not "Would you use this?"

The best conversational extraction questions are:
1. "Tell me about this idea. Where did it come from?"
2. "What happened that made you think 'someone should build this'?"
3. "Who specifically has this problem? Can you name someone?"
4. "When they hit this problem, what do they actually do today?"
5. "What's the worst part about the current way?"
6. "If this existed and worked perfectly, what would change for them?"
7. "Why hasn't someone built this already? What's different now?"
8. "How would they find out about this?"
9. "Would they pay for it? How do you know?"
10. "What's the smallest version of this that would be useful?"

#### The Funnel Pattern

Good extractors follow a funnel: broad → specific → implications.

1. **Broad:** "Tell me about your idea" (let them talk, listen for the real problem)
2. **Narrowing:** "Who specifically?" / "When specifically?" / "What happens specifically?"
3. **Testing:** "Why would they switch from what they do now?" / "What's the risk if you're wrong about this?"
4. **Grounding:** "What's the smallest thing you could build to test this?" / "What would success look like in 6 months?"

## 12. Anti-Patterns

### Questions That Don't Work

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails | Better Alternative |
|-------------|-------------|-------------------|
| "Would you use this?" | Invites optimism, not truth | "When did you last face this problem?" |
| "How much would you pay?" | Hypothetical; people lie | "What do you currently spend on solving this?" |
| "Do you think this is a good idea?" | Invites compliments | "What's the hardest part about [context]?" |
| "What features would you want?" | Customers design bad products | "Walk me through the last time you tried to do this" |
| "Isn't this market huge?" | Leading question | "Who specifically has this problem?" |
| "Don't you hate when [problem]?" | Embeds the answer | "Tell me about [context]" (open-ended) |

### Process Anti-Patterns

1. **Solution-first thinking** — Building backwards from "I want to build X" to justify a problem. Every framework warns against this. Maurya found founders "faking the problem box" to justify pre-decided solutions.

2. **Loving the solution** — Emotional attachment to the product idea prevents honest evaluation. JTBD and Cagan both say: fall in love with the problem.

3. **Asking instead of observing** — "Would you use this feature?" gets you nothing actionable. Customers cannot imagine future products in ways that generate actionable insight (the "psychological interference" problem).

4. **Confirmation-biased discovery** — Engineering the discovery process to validate pre-existing ideas rather than genuinely exploring. Per Cagan: "Discovery is about finding an effective solution to a problem," not rubber-stamping a decision already made.

5. **One-dimensional testing** — Relying only on surveys, or only on A/B tests, or only on user interviews. Good extraction combines quantitative (how many, how often) with qualitative (why, how does it feel).

6. **Big-bang discovery** — Spending months polishing a few experiments instead of testing continuously. Torres: conduct 3-4 interviews, map, then do 3-4 more.

7. **Asking "What do you need?"** — Customers don't know. They know what's frustrating, what they've tried, what didn't work. Extract those things instead.

8. **Premature specificity** — Asking about pricing, features, or technical details before the problem and customer are clear. Per YC: if the problem isn't real, painful, and large, nothing else matters.

9. **Generic customer definition** — "Our target market is everyone" or "small businesses." Every framework demands specificity: which person, in which situation, with which pain?

10. **Skipping current alternatives** — Not understanding what people do TODAY. This is the single most under-asked area. If you don't know the incumbent behaviour, you can't design something better.

## Key Takeaways for Cold Anvil's Conversational Flow

1. **The universal starting point is the problem.** Not "tell me your idea" — "tell me about the problem." Every framework agrees.

2. **The second question is always "who."** Not "who's the market" but "who specifically has this problem, and can you name one?"

3. **Current alternatives are the most undervalued extraction.** Understanding what people do today gives you the competitive landscape, the switching cost, and the bar your solution must clear — all in one question.

4. **Solution should come third or later.** The best frameworks deliberately delay solution discussion. Maurya leaves the solution box blank during problem validation. Torres starts with outcomes and opportunities before solutions.

5. **Stories beat hypotheticals.** Every conversational extraction framework (Mom Test, JTBD, Torres) relies on past-tense, specific, story-based questions. "Tell me about the last time..." is the single most valuable prompt.

6. **Validate through behaviour, not opinion.** Commitments (time, money, reputation) are the only real validation. "That sounds great" is not validation.

7. **The funnel is: Broad story → Specific problem → Specific customer → Current alternatives → Proposed solution → Why better → Why now → How it works commercially.**

8. **Leave room for "I don't know."** The best frameworks (Lean Canvas unfair advantage box, Cagan's iterate principle) explicitly expect blank spaces. A vision document with honest "we need to find out" sections is more valuable than one filled with fiction.
